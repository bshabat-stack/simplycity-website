Local SEO for Small Business

Last updated: July 2026 · By the team managing 200+ local business profiles

Local SEO is how a small business shows up when nearby customers search on Google — in the Map Pack, on Google Maps, and in local results. To make sure your business ranks high, we help with your Google Business Profile category, a steady flow of reviews, the page your profile links to, and consistent business listings.

Your business needs to be visible to the right customers. We make it happen.

Why local SEO is the highest-ROI channel for small businesses

When someone searches “plumber near me” or “best brunch in [city],” they're not researching — they're choosing. Google's own research found that a large majority of people who search for something nearby on their smartphone visit a related business within a day.

Compared to social media ads, where you pay to interrupt people, Local SEO captures demand that already exists. For a business where one customer is worth $50–$500+, a few extra Map Pack appearances per week can cover the cost of your entire channel.

“Local SEO doesn’t create demand — it captures the demand your competitors are currently winning.”

The 5 things that actually move local rankings

Industry studies and our data across 200+ managed profiles agree on what matters. In order:

1
Your Google Business Profile — especially the primary category
Your primary category is the single strongest field on your profile. “Hair salon” vs “Beauty salon” vs “Barber shop” are different ranking universes — pick the one matching what customers actually search. Then complete everything: services with descriptions, accurate hours, attributes, and a real photo set. Google states that complete and accurate business information improves local visibility. Full breakdown of how we do this: Google Business Profile optimization →
2
Reviews — quantity, quality, and recency
Google says directly that more reviews and positive ratings can improve local ranking. The pattern that wins is a steady flow, not bursts: a review system built into your customer journey (ask at checkout, QR code on the counter, follow-up message) beats begging for reviews twice a year. Reply to every review — including the bad ones — with a process.
3
The page your profile links to
Your GBP links to a page on your website, and that page's relevance affects Maps ranking. Link your profile to a page that matches your main search term — title tag, headings, and content — not to a generic homepage that says ‘Welcome.’
4
Consistent listings (citations)
Your name, address, and phone number should be identical everywhere they appear — Google, Apple Maps, Yelp, Facebook, directories. Old addresses and duplicate listings quietly cap your ranking. Clean up what exists before building new listings.
5
A service page for each thing you sell
One page per service (‘emergency plumbing,’ ‘balayage,’ ‘vegan brunch’) outranks one page listing everything. Each page targets its own search, links back to your main page, and gives your profile more relevant landing options.

What you can skip (for now)

Just as important as the list above. These are commonly oversold and consistently rank as low-impact in industry studies:

  • Google Posts — fine for engagement, weak for ranking
  • Profile descriptions — write one, but it's not a ranking lever
  • Q&A seeding, geotagged photos, service-area stuffing — marginal at best
  • Expensive ‘citation blasts’ to 500 directories — a few dozen accurate listings beat hundreds of junk ones

If an agency leads its pitch with any of these, that tells you something.
