Reviews are not just what convinces a customer who already found you. They are part of what determines whether that customer finds you at all.

Most local business owners think about reviews the way they think about a tidy dining room: it matters once someone walks in. The review count sitting next to your name on Google Maps is doing something else entirely. It is one of the inputs Google uses to decide which three businesses appear in the Local Pack — the short list at the top of the results — and which ones sit below the fold where almost nobody scrolls.

That distinction is the whole point of this article. Reviews are a ranking signal, not just social proof. This guide covers what Google actually measures, what a 40-review gap between you and the shop down the street really costs, and how to close one in about 90 days.

## Do Google reviews affect local ranking?

Yes. This is not a contested point among local SEO practitioners, and it is not an inference from correlation studies.

[Google's own guidance on improving your local ranking](https://support.google.com/business/answer/7091) describes three components: relevance, distance, and prominence. Prominence is Google's measure of how well-known a business is — and Google states directly that review count and review score factor into it. More reviews and a higher rating improve local ranking. That is the primary source, published by the company that runs the algorithm.

The independent research agrees on the magnitude. [Whitespark's 2026 Local Search Ranking Factors survey](https://whitespark.ca/local-search-ranking-factors/), which polls dozens of local SEO practitioners on what actually moves rankings, weights review signals at roughly 20% of Local Pack ranking — the second-largest category after Google Business Profile signals at 32%. That share has risen year over year.

Put plainly: about one fifth of what decides your Maps placement is a thing your customers control and most owners never actively manage.

## The four review signals Google measures

"Get more reviews" is where most advice stops, and it is why most review strategies underperform. Google is not reading one number. It is reading at least four, and they behave very differently.

<img src="/blog/google-reviews-ranking-factor/review-signals.jpg" alt="The four Google review signals that affect local ranking" width="1200" height="800" loading="lazy">

### Signal 1: Count

Total review volume matters, with sharply diminishing returns.

The distance between 12 reviews and 60 is enormous — it is the difference between a business Google has little evidence about and one it can place with confidence. The distance between 300 and 350 is close to meaningless. If you are under about 50 reviews, count is likely your biggest single opportunity. If you are over 200, it almost certainly is not, and effort is better spent on the three signals below.

### Signal 2: Rating

Your star average affects ranking, and it affects something arguably more valuable: whether anyone clicks.

Consumer thresholds have tightened dramatically in a single year. According to [BrightLocal's Local Consumer Review Survey 2026](https://www.brightlocal.com/research/local-consumer-review-survey/) — a February 2026 survey of 1,002 US adults — 68% of consumers now require a minimum of 4 stars before they will use a business, up 13 points from 55% the year before. And 31% will only use businesses rated 4.5 or higher, nearly double the 17% who said the same in 2025.

The second-order effect is the one that compounds. A higher rating earns more clicks from the same ranking position, and click-through is itself a behavioural signal that feeds back into ranking. Rating buys you placement and then buys you more placement.

### Signal 3: Velocity and recency

This is the signal owners ignore most and the one that moves fastest.

Google treats a steady flow of new reviews as evidence that a business is currently operating and currently good. A long gap reads as staleness regardless of what the total says.

Customers read it the same way. BrightLocal found that 74% of consumers look for reviews written within the last three months, and 32% want reviews from the last two weeks. A review from 2023 is, to a meaningful share of your market, not a review at all.

The practical consequence is the most encouraging fact in this article: a business with 70 reviews receiving three a week will out-signal a business with 200 reviews that has received nothing since last spring. Velocity is a rate, not a total — which means it is the one input where a smaller business can win starting this week.

### Signal 4: Response rate

Replying to reviews is a ranking-adjacent signal and a conversion one.

BrightLocal's 2026 survey found that 80% of consumers are more likely to use a business that replies to all of its reviews. But the same survey found that generic or templated replies actively put off 50% of them — so the automation shortcut costs roughly as much as it saves.

Replies also tell Google's systems that the profile is actively managed. That matters more than it used to now that [Google's AI reads your reviews](/blog/google-ask-maps) rather than just counting them.

## Your competitor has 40 more reviews than you. Here's the math.

Consider two businesses on the same street, in the same category, both rated 4.6.

| | Your business | Competitor |
|---|---|---|
| Reviews | 38 | 78 |
| Rating | 4.6 | 4.6 |
| New reviews per month | 2 | 6 |
| Typical Local Pack position | Below the pack | Top 3 |

Same street. Same rating. Arguably the same quality. But the review gap puts one of them in the three-result Local Pack and the other on the "more places" screen that most searchers never open.

Now follow what happens next, because this is the part that is usually missed.

<img src="/blog/google-reviews-ranking-factor/review-gap.jpg" alt="How a Google review gap between two competitors compounds over time" width="1200" height="700" loading="lazy">

The competitor ranks higher, so they get more clicks. More clicks means more visits. More visits means more customers who might leave a review — so they collect six a month while you collect two. The gap that was 40 reviews in January is 88 by December.

**The gap is not static. It compounds.** Every month it goes unaddressed, it costs more to close.

Which is exactly why velocity is the lever. At three new reviews a week against a competitor's six a month, a 40-review gap closes in roughly fourteen weeks — about one quarter. Not because you outspent anyone, but because you asked and they assumed.

## What "enough reviews" looks like in your industry

There is no universal number. The only benchmark that matters is the businesses Google is currently ranking above you for your search terms — and the shape of that benchmark is very different by category.

**Restaurants and cafés** have the highest natural review velocity of any local category. Customers review the same day, often before they have left the table. That cuts both ways: the volume is easy to build, but the bar set by the busiest place on your street is high, and a quiet month shows up in the recency signal immediately. Our work with [restaurants and cafés](/local-seo-for-restaurants) usually starts by measuring against the three nearest competitors rather than any national average.

**Beauty and wellness** businesses run at lower volume and higher intent. A salon with 60 reviews can dominate a neighbourhood where restaurants need 200 — because there are fewer of them, each one carries more weight, and the language inside them is unusually specific. Reviews mentioning balayage, gel removal, or deep tissue do double duty as service-relevance signals. See how we approach [beauty and wellness businesses](/local-seo-for-beauty-wellness).

**Retail** is the most under-reviewed category in local search. Customers simply do not think to review a shop the way they review a meal. That is an opportunity rather than a problem: a modest, steady flow of reviews puts a [retail shop](/local-seo-for-retail) ahead of nearly every local competitor, because almost none of them are asking.

## How to close a 40-review gap in 90 days

Five steps. None of them require a marketing budget.

### 1. Measure the actual gap first

Open Google Maps, search your main category plus your neighbourhood, and write down the review count of the three businesses ranking above you. That is your target — a real number attached to real competitors, not an abstract goal. Most owners have never done this and are surprised by how small the gap turns out to be.

### 2. Ask at the moment of satisfaction

Not by email three days later. At the moment the customer is happiest, which is a specific and predictable moment in every business: when the bill arrives and the meal landed; when the chair turns toward the mirror and they see the colour; at the till, bag in hand, when they have found exactly what they came for.

That moment converts several times better than any follow-up message.

### 3. Make it one tap

[Google gives every business a short review link](https://support.google.com/business/answer/3474122). Put it behind a QR code on the table, the mirror, or the counter, and include it in any post-visit message you already send. Every extra step — searching for you, scrolling to find the review button — costs you a meaningful share of the people who genuinely intended to write one.

### 4. Ask for specifics, not stars

"Would you mind mentioning what you had?" is a better request than "would you leave us five stars?"

"Great place, highly recommend" tells Google nothing about what you offer. "Had the balayage and toning — best colour result I've found in Barcelona" contains four separate relevance signals. Specific reviews rank you for specific searches, and they are more persuasive to the next reader.

### 5. Reply to all of them, individually

Every review, positive and negative, within a few days. Reference something particular from what they wrote. Given that templated replies put off half of consumers, this is the one task worth doing by hand — a two-line human reply beats a polished generic one.

One prerequisite underneath all of this: reviews attach to your [Google Business Profile](/google-business-profile-optimization), so the profile needs to be claimed and accurate before any of this compounds.

## Frequently asked questions about Google reviews and ranking

**Do Google reviews affect local ranking?**

Yes. Google's own documentation states that review count and review score contribute to prominence, one of the three factors — alongside relevance and distance — that determine local ranking. Whitespark's 2026 Local Search Ranking Factors survey estimates review signals at roughly 20% of Local Pack ranking weight, the second-largest category after Google Business Profile signals.

**How many Google reviews do I need to rank on Google Maps?**

There is no fixed threshold. The number that matters is relative: how many reviews the businesses currently ranking above you for your search terms have. Search your main category plus your neighbourhood and count the top three results. As a rough guide, restaurants generally need higher volume than salons or clinics, and retail shops typically need the least because so few of their competitors collect reviews at all.

**Do reviews affect ranking more than star rating?**

They work together, but the more useful distinction is between total count and review velocity. Once a business is past roughly 50 reviews, adding to the total produces diminishing returns, while a steady flow of recent reviews keeps producing gains. Rating matters most as a conversion threshold — 68% of consumers require at least 4 stars before they will use a business, so a rating below that suppresses clicks even from a good ranking position.

**How quickly do new Google reviews affect my ranking?**

Individual reviews appear on your profile within hours. Ranking movement from a sustained change in review velocity typically becomes visible over four to eight weeks, and continues compounding as long as the flow is maintained. A single burst of reviews followed by silence produces a much smaller effect than the same number spread steadily over three months.

**Can I ask customers for Google reviews?**

Yes. [Google's policies](https://support.google.com/contributionpolicy/answer/7400114) permit asking customers for reviews. What is prohibited is incentivising them — offering discounts, free items, or entry into a prize draw in exchange for a review — as well as review gating (asking only happy customers) and buying reviews. Asking every customer, without conditions and without reward, is both permitted and the most effective approach.

**Do negative reviews hurt my local ranking?**

A negative review lowers your average rating, which has some effect, but the larger risk is conversion: BrightLocal's 2026 survey found that 77% of consumers say negative reviews make them less likely to use a business. The mitigation is volume and response. A handful of negative reviews inside a large, recent, well-answered set reads as normal. The same reviews inside a set of fifteen, none of them answered, reads as a warning.

**Do reviews on Yelp or TripAdvisor affect my Google ranking?**

Not directly — Google ranks on its own review data. But third-party reviews contribute to overall prominence and are read by AI search tools that draw on the wider web. For local ranking specifically, Google reviews are where effort produces the most measurable return.

## Summary: reviews are placement, not just proof

Google reviews are a local ranking signal worth roughly 20% of Local Pack weight, confirmed by Google's own documentation on prominence. Google measures at least four distinct things: review count (with diminishing returns past roughly 50), star rating (68% of consumers require 4+, 31% require 4.5+), velocity and recency (74% of consumers look for reviews from the last three months), and response rate (80% prefer businesses that reply to everything, but templated replies put off 50%). A review gap between competitors compounds, because higher ranking produces more customers who then leave more reviews. Velocity is the fastest lever available to a smaller business: three reviews a week closes a typical 40-review gap in about one quarter.
