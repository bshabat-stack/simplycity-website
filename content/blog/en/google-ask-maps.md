## What is Google Ask Maps?

Google Ask Maps is a conversational AI search layer built into Google Maps, powered by Google Gemini, launched in March 2026.

For the past decade, finding a local business on Google worked the same way. A user typed "restaurant near me," Google returned a ranked list of three options in the Map Pack, and the user clicked one.

Ask Maps changes that model fundamentally. Instead of returning a list, Ask Maps generates a personalized recommendation in response to natural-language queries. A user opens Google Maps and asks:

> "I need a hair salon near Eixample that specializes in balayage and has appointments on weekends."

Ask Maps reads that request, evaluates every relevant business in the area, and surfaces one to three specific recommendations — named businesses, presented conversationally, with reasons.

The key difference: Google used to rank you. Now it recommends you — or it doesn't.

The businesses Ask Maps recommends are not necessarily the closest, the highest-rated, or the most-reviewed. They are the ones whose Google Business Profiles give the AI the clearest, most specific picture of what they offer and who they serve.

## How Google Ask Maps decides who to recommend

Ask Maps draws on three primary signals to generate recommendations. Understanding these signals is the foundation of appearing in them.

### Signal 1: Your Google Business Profile is the AI's source document

Google Business Profile (GBP) is the primary data source Ask Maps uses to understand and recommend local businesses.

Ask Maps reads a business's GBP the way a person reads a CV: categories, services, products, description, attributes, photos, Q&A, posts, and opening hours — and uses that information to determine whether the business matches a given query.

The more complete and specific a GBP, the more match signals the AI has available.

<img src="/blog/google-ask-maps/signal-1-attributes.jpg" alt="Google Business Profile attributes section showing checkboxes for a restaurant" width="1200" height="800" loading="lazy">

A concrete example: a user searches for "a salon good for colour treatments near Gràcia." If a salon's GBP lists "hair colouring," "balayage," and "highlights" under Services, that salon is a candidate. If the GBP only says "Hair Salon" with no services listed, Ask Maps has no evidence the salon offers colour treatments — and the salon is invisible.

> "The more complete and specific your Google Business Profile, the more signals Ask Maps has to match your business to the right customer query." — SimplyCity

[Google states directly](https://support.google.com/business/answer/7091) that businesses with complete and accurate information are more likely to show up in local search results — and [Whitespark's 2026 Local Search Ranking Factors](https://whitespark.ca/local-search-ranking-factors/) puts Google Business Profile signals, which include completeness, at 32% of Local Pack weight, the largest single category. Ask Maps amplifies this gap further because it needs attribute-level specificity, not just basic information.

### Signal 2: Reviews are AI training data

Ask Maps performs sentiment analysis on Google review text to determine what a business is actually known for — beyond its listed category.

Ask Maps doesn't just count reviews — Google's Gemini AI reads them. When multiple reviews mention "great for a date night," "perfect for big groups," or "the best outdoor terrace in the neighbourhood," the AI treats those recurring phrases as credible, consistent attribute signals for that business.

This means the language of reviews matters as much as the star rating. A 4.6-star business with 60 reviews that specifically mention "outdoor terrace," "quiet atmosphere," and "good for remote work" will outrank a 4.8-star business with 200 reviews that all say "great place, highly recommend" — for any attribute-specific query.

> "Ask Maps reads what your reviews say, not just how many you have — the recurring phrases become the attributes it matches you on." — SimplyCity

Review velocity also matters. [Whitespark's 2026 Local Search Ranking Factors](https://whitespark.ca/local-search-ranking-factors/) puts review signals at roughly 20% of Local Pack weight, and practitioners consistently rank review recency — how recently new reviews were posted — above raw review total. [BrightLocal's Local Consumer Review Survey 2026](https://www.brightlocal.com/research/local-consumer-review-survey/) finds the same pattern among customers: 74% look for reviews written within the last three months.

### Signal 3: GBP attributes are the new ranking factor

GBP attributes — the checkboxes that describe operational details like "outdoor seating," "good for groups," and "dog friendly" — are a direct feed into Ask Maps' recommendation logic.

Each attribute functions as a match signal for attribute-specific queries. A café with "outdoor seating" and "good for remote work" checked will surface when someone asks "café with a terrace where I can work." A café without those attributes set won't appear — even if it has both a terrace and fast WiFi.

GBP attributes are the most underused section in most local business profiles. [Google's guidance on editing your Business Profile](https://support.google.com/business/answer/3039617) shows how many attribute options exist for a given business type — typically far more than an owner sets during initial setup, which is where the opportunity sits.

> "Every unchecked GBP attribute is a query Ask Maps can't match you to." — SimplyCity

## 5 actions to show up in Google Ask Maps

These five actions, taken in priority order, are sufficient to meaningfully improve Ask Maps visibility for any restaurant, café, or salon. None requires technical expertise.

### 1. Complete every section of your Google Business Profile

Google is explicit that complete, accurate profiles are more likely to show up in local results. Start with the three highest-impact fields:

- **Primary category** — This is the single most important relevance signal in a GBP, according to the 47 local SEO practitioners surveyed for [Whitespark's 2026 Local Search Ranking Factors](https://whitespark.ca/local-search-ranking-factors/). Be specific: "Tapas Restaurant" outperforms "Restaurant." "Hair Colouring Salon" outperforms "Hair Salon."
- **Business description** — Google provides 750 characters. Include the business type, neighbourhood, core service, and 1–2 standout attributes. Write naturally: "Cut n'Color is a Barcelona hair salon specialising in colour treatments, balayage, and precision cuts. Located in Eixample, we serve both locals and visitors looking for expert results in a relaxed environment."
- **Name, address, phone, hours** — must be accurate and match every other directory listing. Inconsistencies suppress ranking.

### 2. Set every relevant attribute

GBP attributes are found under Edit Profile → More → Attributes. Work through the full list and check everything that genuinely applies.

For a restaurant: outdoor seating, good for groups, live music, full bar, family-friendly, delivery, takeaway, reservations required, good for special occasions.

For a salon: wheelchair accessible, online booking, women-owned, natural hair care, walk-ins welcome.

Ask yourself: what do customers ask before visiting? Those questions are the attributes to set. Each attribute is a new query Ask Maps can match your business to.

### 3. Generate specific, descriptive reviews

Specific reviews — where customers mention the service received, the atmosphere, or a standout detail — are significantly more valuable to Ask Maps than generic praise. Ask Maps uses recurring phrases across multiple reviews to establish what a business is actually known for.

When requesting a review, try: "Would you mind mentioning what you had or what service you got? It helps other customers know what to expect."

This shifts reviews from "Amazing place, 5 stars!" to "Had the balayage and toning — best colour result I've found in Barcelona. Took about 3 hours and the result was exactly what I wanted." The second review adds at least four attribute signals ("balayage," "toning," "Barcelona," "3 hours") that the first does not.

Reply to every review — positive and negative. [Google itself notes](https://support.google.com/business/answer/7091) that replying to reviews shows you value customer feedback, and that positive reviews and helpful replies help a business stand out.

Target: 2–3 new reviews per week. Review velocity consistently outweighs total review count for local ranking — a business receiving fresh reviews weekly will outrank a competitor with a higher total count but no recent activity.

### 4. List every service and product

Each service or product listing is a keyword-rich data point Ask Maps uses for relevance matching.

- **Salons:** list every service — cut, blow-dry, colour, balayage, highlights, keratin, extensions, toning — with descriptions and price ranges.
- **Restaurants:** add the full menu, or a simplified version (starters, mains, desserts), via GBP's built-in menu feature.
- **Cafés:** list all offerings — specialty coffee, brunch, pastries, vegan options, all-day breakfast.

Profiles with services and products listed are far more visible for service-specific queries, because the AI pulls directly from the Services section when matching a request to available businesses. An empty Services section gives it nothing to match against.

### 5. Post on Google Business Profile weekly

GBP Posts signal active management to Ask Maps. A business that posts weekly is treated as current and engaged; a business that last posted six months ago is treated as potentially inactive.

Format: one photo + 2–3 sentences about a seasonal offer, event, or new menu item + one call to action (book now, call us, visit this week). One post per week is sufficient. The businesses that maintain this habit consistently compound their advantage over competitors who stop after the first month.

## Frequently asked questions about Google Ask Maps

**What is Google Ask Maps?**

Google Ask Maps is a conversational AI search feature inside Google Maps, launched in March 2026 and powered by Google Gemini. It allows users to ask natural-language questions — such as "find me a quiet restaurant near the Sagrada Família good for a business dinner" — and receive AI-generated recommendations for specific local businesses, rather than a ranked list.

**Is Google Ask Maps available in Spain?**

Not yet. Ask Maps [launched on 12 March 2026](https://blog.google/products-and-platforms/products/maps/ask-maps-immersive-navigation/) in the US and India, and as of July 2026 those remain the only markets where it is live. Google has said the feature will expand to more countries but has not announced a date for Spain or the wider EU, where GDPR and DMA requirements make the rollout slower. That gap is an advantage rather than a reason to wait: the profile signals Ask Maps reads are the same ones that already drive Google Maps ranking today, so a business that optimises now is ranking better in the meantime and is ready the day the feature arrives.

**How does Ask Maps decide which businesses to recommend?**

Ask Maps draws on three primary signals: (1) Google Business Profile completeness and specificity — categories, description, services, products, attributes, and posts; (2) review sentiment analysis — the language customers use in reviews to describe a business's attributes; and (3) GBP attributes — the operational checkboxes (outdoor seating, good for groups, dog friendly, etc.) that define a business's characteristics. Businesses that provide detailed signals across all three areas are recommended more consistently.

**Do I need a separate strategy for Ask Maps vs. regular Google Maps?**

No. Ask Maps is powered by the same Google Business Profile data as regular Google Maps. The same optimization steps — completing your profile, setting attributes, generating specific reviews, listing services — improve both your traditional Maps ranking and your Ask Maps visibility simultaneously. Ask Maps gives greater weight to attribute specificity and review language than the traditional local pack, so these areas deserve particular attention.

**How long does it take to see results after optimising for Ask Maps?**

GBP profile changes can produce visible results within days to two weeks. Meaningful ranking improvements in both traditional Maps and Ask Maps typically appear within four to eight weeks of consistent optimisation. Review velocity improvements compound over time — the ranking effect grows the longer a business maintains a steady flow of new, specific reviews.

**My competitor has far more reviews than me. Can I still appear in Ask Maps?**

Yes. Ask Maps matches on multiple dimensions — not just review count. A business with 50 specific, well-responded reviews and a fully completed GBP with detailed attributes can outrank a competitor with 300 generic reviews for attribute-specific queries. Profile completeness and attribute specificity can directly offset a review volume disadvantage.

**Does my Google Business Profile help with ChatGPT or Perplexity recommendations?**

Not directly — ChatGPT and Perplexity do not draw from GBP data in the same way Google does. However, a well-optimised GBP creates consistent entity signals across a business's website, Google's Knowledge Graph, directories, and review platforms. Businesses with strong GBP optimisation tend to have stronger overall web presence, which influences how third-party AI platforms identify and describe them.

**What if I don't have time to manage my Google Business Profile consistently?**

Consistent GBP management — weekly posts, review responses, attribute updates, and monitoring — is the difference between a profile that ranks and one that doesn't.
