If your business is not showing up on Google Maps, the cause is almost always one of eight things: the profile is unverified, it has been suspended, it is marked closed, a duplicate listing is absorbing it, the primary category is wrong, the address or service area is set up incorrectly, your name and address are inconsistent across the web, or — most common of all — the listing is fine and simply ranks below the point where anyone scrolls. Each one has a different fix, and the first job is working out which one you have.

<img src="/blog/business-not-showing-on-google-maps/hero.jpg" alt="A business missing from Google Maps local search results" width="1200" height="630" loading="eager" fetchpriority="high">

"Not showing up" describes two very different problems that owners describe with the same sentence, and conflating them wastes weeks.

The first is invisibility: the listing genuinely is not there. Search your exact business name and nothing comes back. This is a technical or policy problem, and it is usually fixable in days.

The second is obscurity: the listing exists, it appears when you search the name, but it never appears when someone searches for what you actually sell. This is a ranking problem, and it takes weeks rather than days — but it is also the one that has real money attached to it, because nobody searches for your business name until they already know you exist.

This guide covers the diagnosis first, then all eight causes with the fix for each.

## The 60-second diagnosis

Before changing anything, run three checks in this order. They separate the two problems above and narrow eight causes down to one or two.

<img src="/blog/business-not-showing-on-google-maps/diagnosis-flow.jpg" alt="Three checks to diagnose why a business is not showing on Google Maps" width="1200" height="800" loading="lazy">

**Check 1 — Search your exact business name on Google Maps.** Use a logged-out browser or an incognito window; logged in, Google may show you your own profile when the public cannot see it. If the business appears, your listing exists and is live. Skip to reasons 5 through 8. If nothing comes back, you have an invisibility problem — reasons 1 through 4.

**Check 2 — Open your Google Business Profile dashboard.** Google states plainly what the status is: verified, pending verification, suspended, or closed. Most owners never look, and the answer to a fortnight of guessing is often sitting on that screen in a single word.

**Check 3 — Search your category plus your neighbourhood, standing somewhere else.** Not "my business name" — the phrase a customer would use: "hair salon Gràcia", "sandwich shop near me". And do it from a different part of town, because Google Maps results change with the searcher's position. Ranking first from behind your own counter and nowhere from three streets away is not a bug; it is exactly what proximity looks like.

## The 8 reasons your business isn't showing on Google Maps

### Reason 1: The profile was never verified

This is the single most common cause, and the most quietly missed. Creating a Business Profile is not the same as verifying one. Google's documentation is explicit that only verified businesses can show their information on Maps and Search — an unverified profile can sit in the dashboard looking complete and be invisible to every customer.

**The fix:** open the dashboard and complete verification. Google increasingly routes this through video verification rather than a postcard: a single unedited recording showing your signage, the surrounding street, your equipment, and proof you manage the place. Film it in one take, in daylight, and show the exterior before you walk in. Rejections are usually about a missing link in that chain, not about the business being ineligible.

### Reason 2: The profile is suspended or disabled

If a profile violates Google's guidelines it is disabled or suspended, and it stops showing to the public — often with no notification that lands anywhere the owner reads.

2026 has been a heavy year for this. Local search industry trackers have reported repeated mass suspension waves through the spring, with enforcement falling hardest on brand-new listings and on profiles that were recently edited. The common triggers are a keyword-stuffed business name, an address or category that contradicts your other public records, and a burst of rapid edits that trips automated review.

**The fix:** correct the underlying violation *before* filing for reinstatement — a request submitted against an uncorrected profile mostly gets denied. Set the business name to the name on your signage and nothing more, restore your real address, and file the reinstatement form with documentation: a utility bill, a lease, a business registration, photos of the signage. Clean single-profile cases typically resolve in a few days to about two weeks.

### Reason 3: The listing is marked permanently closed

A profile marked closed stays in Google's index but stops appearing in search. This happens after a genuine closure that was never reversed, after a temporary-closure flag from a renovation or holiday that nobody switched back, and — more often than owners expect — after a user-submitted "permanently closed" report that Google accepted.

**The fix:** the dashboard shows the closed status and lets you reopen it. Then check your hours, because empty or wrong hours are a second, subtler version of the same problem: a business showing as closed at the moment of the search is suppressed in results where "open now" is in play, which on mobile is most of them.

### Reason 4: A duplicate listing is absorbing your traffic

If two profiles exist for the same business, Google generally surfaces one — and it is frequently the older, thinner, unmanaged one rather than the profile you have been carefully filling in. Duplicates accumulate from a previous owner, an agency that created a second listing, a relocation, or an auto-generated entry Google built years ago from third-party data.

**The fix:** search your business name and your old addresses on Maps and note every version that appears. Claim the duplicate, then request a merge so the reviews and history consolidate into the profile you keep rather than being deleted along with it. Do not simply delete the duplicate first; the reviews attached to it are worth more than the time it takes to merge properly.

### Reason 5: Your primary category is wrong, or the profile is thin

From here on, the listing exists — it is just losing. Category is the heaviest relevance signal on the profile, and Google matches searches to your *primary* category far more strongly than to the secondary ones. A bakery whose primary category is "Restaurant" competes in the wrong race and loses it.

Completeness compounds the same problem. Missing services, no products, no attributes, no photos and an empty description give Google very little to match a query against, and very little to show an AI-generated answer.

**The fix:** set the primary category to the single most specific one that describes your main business, add secondaries for everything real, then fill the profile out completely — services with descriptions, products, attributes, current photos. This is exactly what our [Google Business Profile optimization](/google-business-profile-optimization) work starts with, because it is the cheapest ranking gain available and most profiles are half-empty.

### Reason 6: Proximity, address, and service-area setup

Distance is one of Google's three stated local ranking factors, along with relevance and prominence — and it is the one you cannot argue with. There is no single Maps ranking; there is a different result for every point on the map. Ranking third from your shop and invisible two kilometres away is normal, not broken.

Setup errors make it much worse. Google's guidance is direct: if you serve customers at their location rather than yours — a plumber, a mobile groomer, a home-based studio — you should hide your address and define a service area instead. Displaying a home address you do not receive customers at is a common suspension trigger. Conversely, hiding the address of a real storefront removes the map pin that pulls in nearby searches.

**The fix:** match the setup to reality. Storefront customers come to → show the address. Service area only → hide it and name up to 20 cities, postal codes or regions, keeping the area inside roughly a two-hour drive of your base rather than claiming a whole country. Both → a hybrid profile, which requires permanent signage and staffed hours. Then check your ranking from several points across your target area, not just from inside the shop.

### Reason 7: Weak prominence — too few, too old reviews

If the profile is verified, correctly categorised and properly placed and still does not appear for category searches, prominence is usually what is missing. Google's own guidance names review count and review score as inputs to it, and Whitespark's 2026 Local Search Ranking Factors survey puts review signals at roughly 20% of Local Pack weight.

The failure mode is rarely a bad rating. It is a small, stale set: forty reviews, none of them from this year, against a competitor collecting six a month. That gap compounds, because the business ranking above you is meeting more customers who then leave more reviews.

**The fix:** ask every customer, at the moment they are happiest, with a one-tap link — and reply to all of them. We cover the mechanics in detail in [do Google reviews affect local ranking](/blog/google-reviews-ranking-factor), including why velocity beats total count and how a typical 40-review gap closes in about a quarter.

### Reason 8: Inconsistent name, address and phone across the web

Google cross-references your profile against the rest of the web. When your address appears three different ways across your website, old directory entries, your Facebook page and a former agency's citations, confidence in the listing drops — and a listing Google is unsure about is a listing it is reluctant to rank.

**The fix:** pick one exact written form of your name, address and phone number, put it on your website in the same words, and correct the directories that carry an old version. The website matters more than the directories: it should carry the same address as the profile, LocalBusiness structured data, and a real page for each service you want to rank for. A profile with nothing behind it plateaus quickly, which is the point where most owners bring in a [local SEO agency](/local-seo-agency) rather than keep guessing.

## "My business disappeared from Google Maps"

A listing that used to show and now does not is a narrower problem than one that never appeared, and it is worth separating.

Ranked yesterday, gone today, name search returns nothing → check for suspension first (reason 2). This is overwhelmingly the cause, and 2026's enforcement waves have made it likelier than in previous years. What triggered it is usually a recent edit rather than anything long-standing: a name changed to add a keyword, an address updated, a category swapped, several fields edited in one sitting.

Still appears by name, but the category rankings vanished → nobody removed you; you were outranked, or an algorithm update reweighted the signals. Work reasons 5 through 8 in order.

Gone only in one part of your service area → almost always proximity or service-area configuration (reason 6), especially if you recently edited your address or areas served.

Marked "permanently closed" without you doing anything → a user report was accepted. Reopen it in the dashboard (reason 3) and it typically restores quickly.

## How long until you show up again?

Verification is the fast one: days, occasionally up to two weeks with video review. Reinstatement after a suspension runs from a few days to about two weeks for a clean, well-documented single profile, and longer where there are multiple profiles, a rebrand, or a prior denial. Profile edits — category, services, hours, address — propagate within hours to a few days.

Once the profile is correct, movement from improved categories, content and review velocity typically becomes visible over four to eight weeks and keeps compounding after that. ## Frequently asked questions about showing up on Google Maps

**Why is my business not showing up on Google Maps?**

Almost always one of eight causes: the Business Profile is unverified, suspended, marked permanently closed, or duplicated; or the profile is live but has the wrong primary category, an incorrect address or service-area setup, too few and too old reviews, or inconsistent business details across the web. Search your exact business name in an incognito window first — if the listing appears, the problem is ranking rather than visibility, and the last four causes are the ones to work through.

**How long does it take for a new business to show up on Google Maps?**

A new profile appears once verification completes, which usually takes a few days and occasionally up to two weeks where video verification is required. Appearing is not the same as ranking: showing up for your own business name happens immediately after verification, while showing up for category searches like "bakery near me" typically takes four to eight weeks of building relevance and prominence.

**Why did my business disappear from Google Maps?**

The most common cause in 2026 is a suspension triggered by a recent edit — a keyword added to the business name, a changed address, or several rapid changes at once. Check your Business Profile dashboard for the status. If the listing still appears when you search its name but no longer appears for category searches, nothing was removed; you were outranked, which is a ranking problem rather than a policy one.

**Why does my business show when I search its name but not for my services?**

Because those are different searches. A name search only requires Google to match the name. A category search asks Google to rank you against every comparable business nearby, using relevance, distance and prominence. A listing that only ever appears by name is usually mis-categorised, thin on services and photos, or short on recent reviews.

**My business shows on Google Maps but not in the top 3 — why?**

The Local Pack shows three results, and which three depends on where the searcher is standing. Check your position from several points across your target area rather than from inside your premises. If you are consistently just below the pack, the gap is usually prominence — review count, review velocity, and the depth of the website behind the profile.

**Do I need a website for my business to show on Google Maps?**

No. A verified Business Profile can appear on Maps with no website at all. But a website materially helps you rank: it is where Google confirms your business details, reads your service pages, and finds the structured data that supports the profile. Profiles with nothing behind them tend to plateau below competitors who have both.

**Why can I see my business on Google Maps but customers can't?**

Because you are logged into the account that owns it. Google shows an owner their own profile in places where the public listing is unverified, suspended, or pending review. Always test in an incognito window or a logged-out browser before concluding that a listing is live.

**Can I fix this myself, or do I need help?**

Verification, reopening a closed listing, correcting categories and merging duplicates are all owner-fixable in an afternoon. Suspensions with a prior denial, multi-location duplicate tangles, and ranking gaps against established competitors are where outside help pays for itself. Start with a diagnosis either way — a [free audit](/audit?source=blog) tells you which of the eight you are dealing with before you spend time on the wrong one.

## Summary

A business missing from Google Maps has either a visibility problem or a ranking problem, and the three-check diagnosis tells you which in about a minute. Visibility problems — unverified profiles, suspensions, closed-marked listings and duplicates — are technical or policy issues, and they resolve in days to two weeks. Ranking problems — wrong primary category, thin profiles, proximity and service-area misconfiguration, weak review prominence and inconsistent business details — resolve over four to eight weeks and are where the commercial upside sits, because category searches are how customers who don't know your name find you. Fix the cause, not the symptom: reinstatement requests filed against an uncorrected profile get denied, and more reviews will not rescue a listing that was never verified.
